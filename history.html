Quantum Computing:

History


1980

Paul Benioff proposed the idea of the Quantum Turing Machine or Quantum Computer. Unlike the classical computuer which functioned on the classical mechanical principle, this one functioned on the quantum mechanical principle.

(Quantumly.com, 2017)


1982

Richard Feynman published a paper named Simulating physics with computers where he proved that quatum systems found in physics cannot be simulated with classical computers.

(Feynman, 1982)

1984

Richard Feynman, in his lecture, showed a Quantum Computer would be able to simulate any quantum system. Including one that would simulate the quantum properties of the physical world.

(Quantumly.com, 2017)



1985

The physicist, David Deustch, put in context the idea of the world's first Universal Quantum Computer. Not only did he show how a quantum computer could reproduce in realizable physical system, but he also showed how a quantum computer could perform certain types of calculations exponentially fast than classical computers. He was the first conceptually explain the Quantum Turing Machine.

(Quantumly.com, 2017)



1994

Peter Shor came up with a Quantum Algorithm that could calculate prime numbers at exponentially faster rate than classical computers. As modern encryptions rely on the fact that classical computers can't factor large prime numbers well this meant that with enough computing power Shor's algorithm could be used to break encryptions.

(Hayward, 2005)



1998

On this year, the first experimental demonstration of a quantum algorithm took place. Oxford researchers used a 2-qubit Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) quantum computer to solve simple computer problems twice as fast as a classical computer.

(Quantumly.com, 2017)




1999

D-Wave formed being the first ever quantum computing company.

(D-Wave Systems, 2018)



2001

Shor’s algorithm was successfully demonstrated by IBM Almaden Research Center in California using a 7 qubit computer, in which they were able to factor 15 in to 5, and 3.

(Quantumly.com, 2017)



2007

D-Wave Systems built the first working 28-qubit quantum computer. It used a chip made at the NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory in California.

(Quantumly.com, 2017)



2010

D-Wave delivered the world’s first commercial quantum computer called the D-Wave One, priced at $10,000,000.

(Quantumly.com, 2017)



2012

A group of Chinese physicists were able to find the factors of the number of 143 using Shor’s algorithm, whereas previously the best was simply the number 21, using D-Wave One.

Also, D-Wave started selling their 512-qubit computer, called Vesuvius. It was bought by Google and installed at NASA’s Ames Research Center.

(Quantumly.com, 2017)



2013

D-Wave attempts to prove their computer is indeed a quantum computer. They published a report of their quantum computer Speed versus a high-end PC. It ended up running an optimization algorithm 3600 times faster than the high-end PC.

(Quantumly.com, 2017)



2017

On March 6, IBMQ was created to create to build commercially available universal quantum computing systems for business and science, which can be accessed through their cloud.

(IBM.com, 2017)

On October 10, 2017 Intel develops their first 17 Qubit chip for their partnered company QuTech.

(Adhikari, 2017)

On Decemeber 10, Microsoft releases the Q# a quantum computing programing language.

(Bright, 2017)



2018

On June 10, Intel begins testing it's Spin Qubit Quantum Computing chip. It is about 50 nanometers across and is the smallest of its kind. Intel is using the same silicon manufacturing techniques it uses to make the classical computer chips.

(Forrest, 2018)

Physicists are able to demonstrate the world's first simulation of a chemical bond using what's known as trapped ion qubits. Trapped ion qubits are one of the more promising pathways to full-scale quantum computing to the degree of which our classical computers are at and more.

(University of Sydney, 2018)



2019

On January 8, IBM unveils IBM Q System One, which is the world's first integrated quantum computing system. This is for both scientific and commercial use.

(IBM, 2019)
